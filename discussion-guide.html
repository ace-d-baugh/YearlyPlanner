PHASE 1: Pre-Meeting Setup 15 minutes

Environment Preparation:

  • Choose neutral, comfortable space (not bedroom, not in front of TV)
  • Eliminate distractions (phones on silent/away, no interruptions)
  • Have water, snacks, tissues available
  • Set time boundaries (block 3-4 hours minimum)
  • Both partners have their completed questionnaires or JSON files
  • Have paper and pen for notes

Ground Rules Agreement:

"Before we begin, let's agree on some ground rules to make this productive:"
  • We will listen fully before responding (no interrupting)
  • We will assume good intentions
  • We will use "I feel..." statements, not "You always..."
  • We will take breaks if emotions escalate
  • We will end with appreciation and next steps
  • We each have ONE "Veto Card" to use
üÉè The "Veto Card" Protocol

Each partner gets ONE "Veto Card" they can play during the session:

"I cannot explain strictly logically why, but this goal/plan gives me visceral anxiety and I cannot commit to it right now. I need us to table this and revisit it in [timeframe]."

Important: Respect the veto without pressuring for explanation. This protects emotional safety when something feels deeply wrong but can't be articulated fully yet.

üìã The "Parking Lot" Agreement

Keep a visible list (whiteboard, paper) for topics that derail progress:

  • If any single topic stalls for >10 minutes, it goes to Parking Lot
  • Schedule dedicated 30-min sessions for Parking Lot items within the next week
  • This prevents one issue from hijacking the entire session

Example Parking Lot items: "What's our actual monthly grocery spend?" "Research retirement calculators" "Get quotes on life insurance"

PHASE 2: Celebrate Alignment 15 minutes
PURPOSE: Build positive foundation before addressing conflicts. Research shows starting with strengths predicts better outcomes.
"Before we discuss differences, let's acknowledge where we're already aligned. This helps us remember we're on the same team."

Activities (in order):

  1. Review üü¢ GREEN pillars together
    • Look at your Alignment Dashboard (if using comparison tool)
    • Or simply compare satisfaction ratings where you're within 2 points
  2. Read aloud one area where you're both highly satisfied
    • Take turns sharing what's going well
  3. Each person shares:
    "Something I appreciate about how you handle [green pillar] is..."
  4. Acknowledge shared goals and values
    • Which goals did you both identify independently?
    • What values showed up in both your lists?
  5. Celebrate prediction accuracy
    "I know you well in these areas - I predicted accurately that you..."
‚ö†Ô∏è DON'T SKIP THIS PHASE!
Starting with positives activates "positive sentiment override" (Gottman research) - you'll interpret differences more charitably and have more emotional resources for harder conversations.
PHASE 3: Understand Differences 40 minutes
PURPOSE: Explore üü° YELLOW flag items (moderate gaps, 3-5 points) to build understanding

Order of Discussion:

  1. Start with üü° YELLOW items (moderate gaps)
  2. Pick 2-3 YELLOW areas that feel most important
  3. Use structured conversation format below

Conversation Format for Each Item (13 minutes per item):

Step 1: Share Perspectives (6 minutes)
  • Partner A explains their rating/response (3 min uninterrupted)
  • Partner B reflects back what they heard (1 min)
  • Partner B explains their rating/response (3 min uninterrupted)
  • Partner A reflects back what they heard (1 min)
Reflection Formula:
"What I hear you saying is... [paraphrase]. The part that seems most important to you is... [core need]. Did I understand correctly?"
Step 2: Identify the Gap Type (2 minutes)

Is this difference due to:

  • Different VALUES (what we think is important)
  • Different PRIORITIES (when we think this matters)
  • Different INFORMATION (what we know/believe)
  • Different NEEDS (what fulfills us)
  • Different EXPERIENCES (our history shapes this)
Step 3: Explore Creative Solutions (5 minutes)

Guiding Questions:

  • "Is there a way to honor both perspectives?"
  • "What would a compromise look like?"
  • "Could we experiment with both approaches?"
  • "Is this an area where difference is actually complementary?"

Example Questions to Deepen Understanding:

"Help me understand why this matters so much to you..."

"What would it feel like for you if we did it your way? My way?"

"What are you afraid might happen if we don't align here?"

"What's the underlying need beneath your preference?"
PHASE 4: HALFTIME BREAK 20-30 minutes ‚ö†Ô∏è MANDATORY
üö® THIS BREAK IS NON-NEGOTIABLE üö®
Do NOT skip this break, even if you feel like you're "on a roll."

Rules:

  • Do NOT discuss the planning session during break
  • Eat something (decision fatigue is real; glucose helps)
  • Move your body (walk, stretch, different room)
  • Do something distinct (music, fresh air, check on kids)
  • Reset emotional state before continuing
Why This Matters

Research on decision fatigue shows decision quality drops precipitously after 90-120 minutes without a break. You will make better decisions about your life if you rest first.

Your prefrontal cortex (rational brain) needs glucose and rest. Without this break, you're more likely to:

  • Make defensive choices
  • Agree to things you don't actually want
  • Get stuck in circular arguments
  • Miss creative solutions
PHASE 5: Navigate Conflicts 45 minutes
PURPOSE: Address üî¥ RED flag items (large gaps >6 points, especially on Mandatory questions)
Critical Mindset:
"These are areas where we see things very differently. That doesn't mean one of us is wrong. Our job is to find a path forward that doesn't require either of us to violate our core values or needs."

Approach for Each RED Item (20 minutes per major conflict):

Step 1: Full Hearing (10 minutes)
  • Partner A: "Here's what this means to me and why it matters..." (5 min uninterrupted)
  • Partner B: Reflects back without judgment (2 min)
  • Partner B: "Here's my perspective and concerns..." (5 min uninterrupted)
  • Partner A: Reflects back without judgment (2 min)
Step 2: Identify Deal-Breakers vs. Preferences (5 minutes)

Each person answers honestly:

  • Is this a DEALBREAKER? (core value, can't compromise)
  • Is this a STRONG PREFERENCE? (important but negotiable)
  • Is this a WEAK PREFERENCE? (I have an opinion but flexible)
Step 3: Problem-Solve (10 minutes)

If both are dealbreakers:

  • This is a fundamental incompatibility that may require professional help
  • Options: couples therapy, trial period, deeper values exploration
  • Be honest about whether this is relationship-threatening

If one is dealbreaker, one is preference:

  • The dealbreaker takes precedence
  • Explore: "What would make this easier for the non-dealbreaker partner?"
  • Compensation: "What can I offer you in another area?"

If both are preferences:

  • Compromise, alternate, experiment, or create hybrid approach
  • Set trial periods: "Let's try it your way for 3 months, then reassess"

Step 4: When Stuck - Use These Tools

Tool How to Use
The "Third Entity" Reframe Ask: "What does our RELATIONSHIP need?" (not just what each person wants). Sometimes both people's preferences need to yield to what serves the partnership.
Values Clarification "What value is driving your position?" (security, freedom, growth, etc.)
"Can that value be honored through a different approach?"
Future Self Perspective "If we're looking back on this in 5 years, what decision would we be proud of?"
"What would 70-year-old us advise?"
Temporary vs. Permanent "Is this a forever decision or can we revisit in 1-2 years?"
Many conflicts ease when reframed as experiments, not permanence.

PHASE 6: Commit to Goals 30 minutes
PURPOSE: Translate insights into actionable commitments

A. Individual Goals Review (10 minutes)

  1. Each person shares their Top 3 personal 1-year goals
  2. Partner responds: "Here's how I can support you..."
  3. Identify any conflicts between individual goals
  4. Agree on what accommodations may be needed

B. Couple Goals Selection (15 minutes)

Critical Process:
  1. Each person shares their Top 3 couple goals
  2. Identify overlapping goals (these become primary)
  3. For non-overlapping goals, discuss:
    • Is one person more invested than the other?
    • Does it truly serve "us" or is it one person's preference?
    • Can it be reframed or modified to create genuine buy-in?
The Shared Vision Test

For each proposed couple goal, both partners answer honestly:

  • "Do I genuinely want this?" (not just willing to go along)
  • "Will I feel resentful if I invest time/energy in this?"
  • "Does this serve our relationship or just one of us?"

If either partner answers "no" to #1 or "yes" to #2, it's not a true couple goal yet.

Select Top 3 Couple Goals for 1-Year (must have genuine buy-in from both)

C. Schedule the Monthly "Board Meetings" ‚ö†Ô∏è MANDATORY

BEFORE ENDING THIS SESSION, SCHEDULE:
  • Next 3 monthly review dates (put in calendar NOW)
  • Format: 45-minute "Board Meeting" to review progress
  • Location: Same neutral space
  • Non-negotiable: If it's not on the calendar, it won't happen
Monthly Board Meeting Structure (45 minutes):
  1. Review goal progress (15 min)
  2. What's working? What's not? (10 min)
  3. Do we need to adjust any goals? (10 min)
  4. What support do we need from each other? (5 min)
  5. Appreciate efforts and progress (5 min)
WHY THIS IS MANDATORY:
The #1 failure point in annual planning is lack of review. Plans without review die by March. Research shows that accountability check-ins increase goal achievement by 65%+. Scheduling the first 3 sessions NOW ensures follow-through.
PHASE 7: Process & Appreciation 15 minutes

Reflection Questions (each person answers):

  • "What did you learn about your partner today?"
  • "What did you learn about yourself?"
  • "What surprised you most?"
  • "Where do you feel most hopeful?"
  • "Where do you feel most concerned?"

Appreciation Protocol:

Each person completes these sentences:

"Thank you for being willing to..."

"I appreciate how you..."

"One thing that gives me confidence in us is..."

"I'm excited about..."

Closing Ritual:

  • Physically mark the end (handshake, hug, toast)
  • Acknowledge the work you've both done
  • Express commitment to the process
  • Agree on one small thing to do together in the next 24 hours
Congratulations! You've completed a comprehensive planning session. This is relationship work at its best.

Post-Session: The Parking Lot Follow-Up

Within 7 Days, Address Parking Lot Items:
  • Schedule 30-minute focused sessions for each item
  • One topic per session (prevents overwhelm)
  • Use same structured format as main session
  • These may require research, consultation, or creative thinking time

Example Session: "Let's spend 30 minutes researching retirement calculators together and then discuss what we learned."

Troubleshooting Common Challenges

‚ùå Problem: One partner dominates the conversation

Solution: Use a timer. Each person gets equal, uninterrupted time. The listener's only job during their partner's time is to listen and reflect back.

‚ùå Problem: We keep going in circles on the same issue

Solution: This is a Parking Lot item. Write it down, schedule a dedicated session for it, and move on. You may need outside perspective or more information.

‚ùå Problem: Emotions are running too high

Solution: Take a break immediately. Walk separately for 15 minutes. Come back when regulated. If it happens repeatedly on the same topic, this may require professional help.

‚ùå Problem: We can't agree on a major issue

Solution: First, identify if it's truly a dealbreaker or a strong preference. If dealbreaker vs. dealbreaker, acknowledge this honestly and consider whether you need couples therapy. Many "dealbreakers" soften when underlying needs are understood.

‚ùå Problem: We're exhausted and want to quit

Solution: This is normal! Did you take your halftime break? If yes, you can end here and schedule a continuation session within 3 days. Don't push through exhaustion‚Äîit leads to poor decisions.

‚ùå Problem: One person feels like they're always compromising

Solution: This is a serious pattern. Track who "won" on each decision. Healthy relationships have balance over time. If pattern persists, couples therapy can help address power dynamics.

When to Seek Professional Help

Consider couples therapy if:
  • You can't get through this session without frequent escalation
  • One partner shows contempt, stonewalling, or chronic defensiveness
  • There are fundamental disagreements on children, fidelity, or finances
  • One partner feels consistently unheard or invalidated
  • You've had the same argument for months/years with no resolution
  • There's been infidelity, betrayal, or broken trust
  • You're considering separation but want to try first
  • Mental health issues are affecting the relationship
Finding the Right Help:
  • Couples therapist: Gottman-trained or Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT) are evidence-based
  • Financial therapist: For money conflicts (find via Financial Therapy Association)
  • Career counselor: For work-life balance or career transition issues
  • Religious/spiritual advisor: For faith-based guidance if that's your framework

Remember: Seeking help is a sign of strength and commitment, not failure.

Appendix: Quick Reference Scripts

When You Need to Take a Break:

"I'm feeling overwhelmed/emotional right now. Can we take a 15-minute break? I want to give this conversation my best, and I need a few minutes to reset."

When You Feel Unheard:

"I don't feel like you're hearing what I'm trying to say. Can you reflect back what you heard me say, so I can clarify?"

When You're About to Get Defensive:

"I notice I'm getting defensive. Let me take a breath and try to hear what you're really saying beneath the words."

When You Want to Use Your Veto Card:

"I need to use my Veto Card. I can't fully explain why right now, but this plan creates visceral anxiety for me. Can we table this and come back to it in [timeframe]?"

When Something Goes to the Parking Lot:

"We've been on this topic for 10 minutes and aren't making progress. Let's add it to the Parking Lot and schedule a dedicated session for it. I'm writing it down now."

When You Need to Repair After Conflict:

"I'm sorry I [specific behavior]. I was feeling [emotion] and I didn't handle it well. What I meant to communicate was [core message]. Can we try this conversation again?"

When You Want to Acknowledge Your Partner:

"I really appreciate that you [specific action]. It made me feel [specific feeling]. Thank you for that."
